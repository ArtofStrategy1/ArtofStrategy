Science and Art of Strategy

By LTC Gurbachan Singh

"War is a science covered in darkness, in the midst of which one does not walk with an assured step… all the sciences have principles, but that of war as yet there are none." — Marshall de Saxe, 1757

The unprecedented successes of the Gulf Wars in 1990 and 2003 fostered a notion that wars could be fought and won through an overwhelming superiority in science, systems, and technology. However, warfare is a complex phenomenon that cannot be successfully waged through science alone. While science and technology change how warfare is conducted, the nature of war remains largely unchanged, requiring a balance of scientific principles and the intuitive “art” of the commander.

The Push for Science and Systems

The 17th and 18th Century Age of Enlightenment spurred the belief that human reason and the scientific method could build a better world. This thirst for order stems from a primordial fear of the unknown. In warfare, this manifested as a desire to reduce war to immutable principles.

History shows that science often dictates the victor: Archimedes held off the Romans with inventions, the English longbow dominated the Scots, and Germany’s blitzkrieg integrated airpower and tanks to defeat France. Modern thinkers like John Boyd and John Warden advocated systems approaches, such as the OODA Loop (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act) to achieve strategic paralysis.

The Principles of War: Laws or Guides?

Antoine-Henri Jomini, inspired by Napoleon, argued for fundamental principles of war that ensure success, such as throwing the mass of forces upon decisive points. Today, the U.S. Army utilizes nine principles of war, including mass, objective, offensive, surprise, and simplicity.

However, these principles are not immutable laws like those in physics. For example, the principle of “mass” (overwhelming force at a decisive point) is subjective; Napoleon’s loss at Waterloo could be attributed to a failure of mass, a failure to attack the decisive point, or simply the weather. Therefore, principles should serve as useful guides and checklists rather than silver bullets.

The Art of War: Navigating Uncertainty

Despite the drive for scientific precision, Jomini acknowledged that “War in its ensemble is not a science, but an art.” Clausewitz described war as a “paradoxical trinity” composed of primordial violence, chance, and reason. In this realm of fear and friction, where information is often false or contradictory, scientific certainty is impossible.

This environment requires “coup d'oeil” — the intellect to see the truth in the darkest hour and the courage to follow it. Immanuel Kant suggested that while “understanding” comes from scientific concepts, “reflective judgment” is an invention of the thinker. It is this reflective judgment that constitutes the art of war: the intuitive weighing of the situation to derive novel plans.

Conclusion

Warfare is ultimately a human endeavour. While plans can be guided by the science of strategy, they must be augmented by leadership and a realistic understanding of uncertainty. Innovations provide advantages, but they are often short-lived. Consequently, the art of war — dominated by intangible factors like courage, morale, and intuitive judgment — will continue to reign supreme alongside scientific advancements.
